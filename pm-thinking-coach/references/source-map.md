## Source Ladder

For any current product/company/industry analysis, collect sources in this order:

1. Official source: launch note, company blog, developer docs, help docs, investor or public strategy material.
2. Operator/expert source: interview, talk, newsletter, founder/PM essay, academic lab page.
3. Practitioner source: 人人都是产品经理, product community writeups, competitor teardown.
4. Social/video source: YouTube/X posts only when directly accessible; use as signals, not proof.

Always separate:

- `source fact`: what the source states.
- `product inference`: the PM judgment drawn from facts.
- `coaching prompt`: what the user should think through personally.

## Book-Based Thinking Foundations

Use these as conceptual anchors. Do not invent exact quotes unless verified from a reliable source.

- `Thinking, Fast and Slow`: System 1/System 2, base rates, loss aversion, availability, anchoring, overconfidence.
- `Influence`: reciprocity, commitment/consistency, social proof, authority, liking, scarcity, unity.
- `Scarcity`: bandwidth tax, tunneling, slack, scarcity traps.
- `The Fogg Behavior Model`: behavior happens when motivation, ability, and prompt converge.
- `Skin in the Game` / asymmetric risk: incentives, downside exposure, fragility, hidden optionality.
- `Critical Thinking` / `Asking the Right Questions`: claim, reasons, evidence, assumptions, ambiguity, alternative explanations.
- `Decision Thinking`: options, tradeoffs, probabilities, second-order effects, pre-mortem, reversibility.

## Source Quality Rules

- Prefer dated pages and current product versions.
- Use at least one primary source before making a strong claim about a modern product.
- Treat social posts as weak signals, not evidence.
- For YouTube, include title/channel/date when visible and summarize only the relevant idea.
- For X, include account and post link when accessible; otherwise say it could not be verified.
- If a source cannot be opened, do not cite it as evidence.
